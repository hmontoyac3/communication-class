This is why Sweet Ballz failed even though the product was fine.

The Sender–Receiver Gap

Where Distortion Enters

→You have an intention
→You encode it into words + body
→It travels through a channel (+ noise)
→They decode it through their own filters
→They interpret it based on their context
≠Their understanding ≠ your intention
The big insight: The gap between what you intend and what they understand is your responsibility to close. Not theirs. The sender owns the effectiveness of the communication. Every great communicator understands this.
THE BRIEF
A real-world communication exercise. The professor will read the brief once. Your job: capture it.
⚠ Professor reads this aloud. Class listens. No recording. No notes until it's over.
Incoming message from your manager · Read once · No replay
"Alright, listen carefully — I'm only saying this once. Our product team has flagged an issue with the Milan launch. The local influencer campaign isn't performing and we need a fix before Q3 closes. Sofia from marketing has already spoken to two agencies — the second one, Vivid Creative, looks promising but they're quoting €18K, which is over budget. The CFO said €15K max unless we can justify ROI clearly. Sofia needs an answer by Thursday.

Meanwhile, logistics in Rome — talk to Marco — needs the final SKU list no later than three weeks before launch. Work backwards from the launch date. Also, the CEO is presenting to the board on the 20th and needs a one-pager: key numbers and the go-to-market narrative. Keep it simple.

One more thing: legal needs to sign off on any campaign assets before they go live, so loop them in early. And the regional managers in the north need to align on the rollout — they've been left out and they're not happy about it.

Can you pull all of this together?"
Read at normal speaking pace. Don't slow down or over-emphasize. That's the point. After reading: "Now, without speaking to your group — write down what you remember. 2 minutes."

Q1 · INFORMATION OVERLOAD
Why did nobody get everything?
The brief had too many items, too many people, too many deadlines — all at the same priority level. When everything is urgent, nothing is. Our brains drop items under information overload. This happens in every company every day — in meetings, in Slack, in emails.
Q2 · SELECTIVE PERCEPTION
What did different people in your group capture that the others didn't?
We hear what our background prepares us to hear. Finance students catch the €15K. Marketing students catch the agency name. People from northern Italy catch the regional manager issue. Same message — completely different interpretation. This is selective perception.
Q3 · CHANNEL CHOICE
Would this brief have been better as an email?
Yes — for complex, multi-action messages with deadlines and names, written is better. Verbal 1-way works for simple urgent messages (the Hawaii missile alert example). Matching channel to message complexity is a core management skill.
Q4 · THE REAL LESSON
Who is responsible when the message doesn't arrive?
The sender. Always. In a company, if the person who gave you the brief is annoyed that you missed something — that's on them. The most effective communicators know this: you check for understanding, you choose the right channel, you repeat what matters, you ask "what did you hear?"
THEORY FLASH 2
Four barriers. Four ways to beat them. Click each card.
Selective Perception
We hear what we expect to hear
Your background, assumptions, and current concerns act as filters. Two people in the same meeting can leave with opposite interpretations. You heard "Sofia needs an answer." They heard "get the SKU list to Marco."
Information Overload
Too much → the brain drops things
When inflow exceeds processing capacity, important info gets lost. 6 action items in 90 seconds, at normal pace, with no context = most of them gone. Every team meeting runs this risk.
Assumptions
We fill gaps with our own version of reality
When we don't fully understand, we don't ask — we guess. And we're often wrong. "The 20th" — which month? Which year? Which time zone? We assume we know.
Emotions & Stress
How you feel changes what you hear
A stressed or distracted receiver misses up to 40% more. The manager who delivers a brief at the end of a meeting when everyone is already half-checked-out gets the worst results.
How great communicators beat these barriers
🔁
Summarize backAfter receiving complex info, say: "So what I heard is…" This closes the gap immediately.
✂️
Less is moreOne clear message beats five scattered ones. Pick the most important thing and make sure THAT lands.
📡
Match channel to messageComplex, multi-step info → written. Urgent, simple → verbal. Sensitive → face to face.
🎯
Know your audienceWhat do they already know? What do they care about? Start from where they are, not where you are.
