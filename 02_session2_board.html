THE JOBS TEST
January 9, 2007. Steve Jobs introduces the iPhone. You're watching 90 seconds. Pay attention to everything.

What he said

The Story Before the Product

He doesn't open with specs or features. He opens with a problem: "The most advanced phones out there are called smartphones — but the problem is they're not so smart." He sets up a villain before revealing the hero. Your pitch needs a villain too.
How he said it

Silence as a Tool

He pauses. Deliberately. Three beats before the reveal. Most presenters are terrified of silence — they fill it with "uhm." Jobs weaponized silence. It creates anticipation. The audience leans in. The reveal lands harder.
His body

He Owns the Stage

He moves with intention, not nervousness. Eye contact is specific — he picks one person at a time. No fidgeting, no locked feet. And he smiles when he means it, not as a habit. His body says: "I know something you're about to love."
The structure

Rule of Three, Every Time

"An iPod. A phone. And an internet communicator." Three things. Repeated. People remember in threes. Every good pitch uses rule of three — your problem, your solution, your ask. Not four. Not two. Three.
The audience

He Made Them Feel Smart

The audience started laughing and cheering before the reveal. He let them figure it out. He said "are you getting it?" — not to condescend, but to confirm they were ahead of him. When the audience feels clever, they're yours.
The lesson for your pitch

You Already Have This

Jobs didn't have a trick. He had preparation and obsession with the audience's experience. For Shark Tank: What's your villain? What's your reveal? What's the one thing you want them to remember? Start there — not with features, not with slides.

WHAT MAKES A PITCH

Every great pitch — Shark Tank, job interview, investor meeting — follows this structure. In 3 minutes, you can hit all 5. Practice it today.

1

The Hook — 20 seconds

Start with the problem, not the product. Make the audience feel the pain before you offer the cure. Example: "Every year, 3 million kilos of food go to waste while 800 million people go hungry. We built the fix."

2

The Solution — 30 seconds

One sentence. What do you do, for whom, and what's different? Avoid jargon. If your grandmother can't understand it, start over. The simpler it sounds, the harder it was to write.

3

The Proof — 60 seconds

Why you? Why now? One piece of evidence that this is real: a user stat, a market number, an early result. Sharks don't fund ideas — they fund traction and people. Show them both.

4

The Ask — 20 seconds

Be specific. "We're asking for €100,000 for 15% equity." Not "some investment." Not "open to discussion." Confidence in your number signals you've done the math. Vagueness signals fear.

5

The Close — 10 seconds

What happens if the sharks invest? Paint the picture. One sentence on the future. Then stop talking. The biggest mistake pitchers make: they keep going after the ask. Stop.

Amy Cuddy's research at Harvard: people judge you on two dimensions within seconds. The order matters.

← Low WarmthHigh Warmth →
High competence · Low warmth

The Envied Expert

Respected but not liked. People listen but won't go out of their way for you.

Risk: Sharks see you as smart but cold — hard to trust with their money.

High competence · High warmth

The Leader

This is the zone. People admire you AND root for you. You get the benefit of the doubt on mistakes.

Target: Competent enough to be credible, warm enough to be fundable.

Low competence · Low warmth

The Threat

People avoid, undermine, or dismiss. The danger zone.

In a pitch: Not knowing your numbers AND being arrogant = instant no.

Low competence · High warmth

The Well-Meaning Amateur

Liked but not taken seriously. People want to help you but won't bet on you.

Shark Tank trap: Being too friendly without proving you can execute.

The sequence: Warmth comes first. Always. Cuddy's finding: competence claims land better once the audience trusts you. Lead with story, smile, energy — then hit them with the data.
What you say
7%

The Words

Just the words. The actual content. If all they get is the transcript, they get almost nothing. Choose them well — but know they're not the message.

How you say it
38%

Voice & Tone

Pace, volume, pitch, pauses. A slow voice signals confidence. A fast voice signals nerves. Deep breath before high-stakes moments. Your voice is an instrument — tune it.

What your body says
55%

Body Language

Posture, eye contact, gestures, space. This is the majority of your message. You can't fake it under pressure — but you can prepare it. Choose your stance before you walk in.

Primacy & Recency — The Memory Curve

People remember the beginning and end of what you say. The middle gets compressed. Design your pitch accordingly.
